# Vector 1: Founder-Voice Consulting Sites That Work

**Question:** What are specific examples of consulting firms or solo practitioners where the founder's personal voice IS the website?
**Agent dispatched:** 2026-03-21

## Findings

### Specific Named Examples

**1. Amy Hoy (Stacking the Bricks)** — stackingthebricks.com
- Former developer, built consulting/education business around product creation
- Voice is blunt, opinionated, loaded with specific references to her own failures
- Pattern: States a common belief, then dismantles it with a specific counter-example from her own experience

**2. Patrick McKenzie (patio11 / Kalzumeus)** — kalzumeus.com
- Solo consultant known for deeply specific writing about software business economics
- Voice is pedagogical but never condescending — teaches by showing his actual reasoning process, including where he was wrong
- Extreme specificity: doesn't say "I help companies with pricing" — writes 5,000 words about how one SaaS company left $2M on the table with a specific pricing antipattern, then mentions he consults on this

**3. Lara Hogan** — larahogan.me
- Engineering leadership coach/consultant, former VP Eng at Etsy and Kickstarter
- Warm but structurally rigorous — publishes actual frameworks with specific career stories
- Gives away methodology publicly. Consulting pitch is implicit: "If you found this useful, imagine what working together looks like"

**4. Mandy Brown** — aworkinglibrary.com
- Design/leadership consultant, former VP Engineering at Vox Media
- Site is literally a library of reading notes and essays — consulting is almost an afterthought structurally
- Writes about intersection of labor, technology, and organizational design with a literary essayist's voice

**5. Tom Critchlow** — tomcritchlow.com
- Independent strategy consultant, former Google
- Publishes actual engagement notes (anonymized), writes about emotional reality of solo consulting
- Coined useful frameworks ("the strategic independent") from lived experience, not theory
- Site reads as a working notebook, not a brochure

**6. Brie Wolfson (The Knotwork)** — theknotwork.com
- Organizational design consultant, former Stripe
- Specific about the gap between how companies say they work and how they actually work
- Names real patterns she observed at specific (named) companies

**7. Charity Majors** — charity.wtf
- CTO of Honeycomb, personal blog functions as consulting-voice example
- Aggressively specific about what she believes is wrong with observability practices
- Willing to be unpopular. Unmistakable voice

**8. Jonathan Stark** — jonathanstark.com
- Pricing/positioning consultant for solo practitioners
- Entire site built around a single, specific, arguable opinion ("hourly billing is nuts")
- Voice works because it has a clear enemy and a clear thesis. You know within 10 seconds what he believes

## Patterns Across Examples

1. **Specificity as credibility signal** — None say "I help organizations navigate transformation." They say exactly what they did, where, when, and what they learned. Specificity IS the trust mechanism — it can't be faked.

2. **Methodology is public; application is the product** — They all give away frameworks for free. Consulting pitch is "you've seen how I think; hire me to think about YOUR problem."

3. **Working notebook > polished brochure** — Most compelling sites read like someone's actual thinking process. Rough edges are features.

4. **Named enemies and clear positions** — Each has a specific thing they believe is broken and says so directly. Not "we take a different approach" but "this common practice is wrong, and here's why."

5. **Former-practitioner authority, not consultant authority** — Every example leads with "I built/shipped/led this specific thing" not "I advise companies on."

6. **The "about" page does the heaviest lifting** — Most compelling copy is biographical and specific. Not "15 years of experience" but the specific story.

7. **Tone matches actual speech** — Could imagine each of them saying their copy out loud in a conversation.
