# Vector 3: How Expertise Communicates Without Credentials

**Question:** How do practitioners — mountain guides, craftspeople, mentors — communicate competence through experience rather than credentials?
**Agent dispatched:** 2026-03-21

## Findings

### Key Principles

**1. Specificity is the mechanism of earned authority**
- Patrick Thomas ("Specificity is Credibility"): vague communication reveals shallow understanding. Competitors all identify "the most obvious change" and stop there, producing identical positioning. The deeper you go, the more specific you get, the more trust you earn.
- Jonah Berger research: concrete language ("at your door" vs "there") increased customer spending by 30%. Concrete language creates trust; abstract language creates vision.

**2. Experts transcend procedures, not cite them**
- Jeffrey Treem ethnographic research: expertise was attributed to people who (1) transcended established procedures, (2) created specialization opportunities, (3) handled large info volumes, (4) shared unsolicited information. Notably absent: citing credentials.
- Gary Klein's Recognition-Primed Decision Making: experts describe *what they noticed*, not *what methodology they applied*. "The snow had a hollow sound" not "we applied the extended column test."

**3. Mountain guides communicate through honest self-correction**
- Will Gadd (30+ years professional climbing): "I've been alpine climbing for more than 30 years, and I learn something new every time I go out." Admitting perpetual learning *strengthens* authority.
- Gadd uses mechanism language: "It's generally safe to be on still-frozen but southwest-facing snow at 6:00 a.m. But put some sun on that slope and you're in the rockfall mixer by 13:00." Authority is in the *mechanism*, not the rule.

**4. Craftspeople describe a dialogue with materials, not mastery over them**
- Ervin Somogyi (master luthier): describes expertise as "eye-hand coordination, trained musculature, critical thinking, intuition, timing, aesthetic sense, dexterity, knowledge of the materials." Not certifications or years.
- Richard Sennett (*The Craftsman*): "A skilled carpenter doesn't just know wood grain patterns abstractly; they feel how different woods respond to pressure."
- Jon Kolko: "The goal of craftsmanship is to get it right, not to get it fast." Craft = "any process that attempts to create a functional artifact without separating design from manufacture."

**5. Yvon Chouinard (Patagonia) — founder-practitioner voice**
- "I never set out to be a businessman. I started as a craftsman, making climbing gear for my friends and myself."
- On sustainability: "Everything man does creates more harm than good. We have to accept that fact and not delude ourselves." Stating an uncomfortable truth plainly because you've thought about it enough to own the conclusion.
- "Would rather be in the woods than in an interview." Authority from visibly preferring the work to the performance of the work.

### Language Patterns

| Pattern | What it sounds like | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism over rule | "Southwest-facing snow destabilizes after 13:00 because sun angle accelerates freeze-thaw" | Only someone who has been there knows the mechanism |
| The specific wrong | "The agent sent 400 emails at 3am because nobody defined behavioral constraints" | Specific wrongs are stories only experienced people tell |
| Self-correction narratives | "I used to think X, then I saw Y, now I think Z" | Demonstrates *updated* knowledge from contact with reality |
| Casual precision | "Rockfall mixer" not "dangerous rockfall zone" | Casualness signals this is how you *think*, not how you *present* |
| Named trade-offs | "We could have done X but Y was more important because Z" | Only someone who made the decision can name the trade-off |
| Inclusive we | "For all of us wanting to improve" | The guide walks *with* you, not ahead of you |
| The scar, not the wound | "We saw it coming. Not as prophecy, but as pattern recognition" | Experience metabolized into understanding |

### Anti-Patterns: What Fake Authenticity Sounds Like

- **Stats-as-credentials:** Using industry statistics as substitute for experience. A guide doesn't start with avalanche fatality statistics. They say "the snow sounded hollow this morning, so we're taking the ridge instead."
- **The "artisanal" trap:** Large corporations adopting craft language without craft practice. "We handcraft governance solutions" vs "We define behavioral constraints in YAML files." The former performs craft; the latter does it.
- **Forced vulnerability:** Sharing from the wound, not the scar. Brene Brown's "Floodlighting Test": "Am I sharing this to be understood, or because I understand it?"
- **Meta-claims of authenticity:** "We believe in honesty and transparency" is a claim. "We would rather lose an engagement than win it with inflated promises" is a named trade-off that *demonstrates* honesty.
- **Negative positioning:** "Unlike other consultancies, we..." tells you nothing about the speaker and everything about their insecurity.

## Implications

**The Foundation Stone already has the voice.** It has conviction, named trade-offs, mechanism over rule, and specific tests ("on an ordinary Tuesday"). The v1 copy was generated from guidelines instead of from the founder's actual perspective.

**The gap is between "warm competence" and "I've been here before."** Current positioning is warm-competent ("Here's what we've seen work") but one level too abstract. What *specifically* have you seen work? On what mountain? In what weather?

**Current anti-patterns in Sherpa's own copy:**
1. Stats block (91%, 95%, 9%) performs expertise through borrowed authority rather than demonstrating it through specific experience
2. "We use what we ship" is right insight stated as proof point — a craftsperson doesn't announce "I use the tools I make"
3. "Ship AI workflows you can actually trust" fails the Headline Test the company's own guidelines define
4. Copy addresses reader as problem-holder rather than joining them as fellow practitioner navigating same terrain
