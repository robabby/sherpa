# Iteration 1 — 2026-03-21

## Findings

### Vector 1: Founder-Voice Consulting Sites That Work
**Question:** What are examples of consulting firms where the founder's personal voice IS the website?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-1-founder-voice-consulting-sites.md](iteration-1/vector-1-founder-voice-consulting-sites.md)

- 10 named examples analyzed (Amy Hoy, Patrick McKenzie, Lara Hogan, Tom Critchlow, Charity Majors, Jonathan Stark, others)
- Pattern: methodology is public, application is the product. Every successful example gives away frameworks freely.
- Strongest pattern: specificity as credibility signal. None say "I help organizations navigate transformation" — they name exact situations, outcomes, and mistakes.
- Sites read like working notebooks, not brochures. Rough edges are features.

**Implications:** The framework story needs to be autobiography, not product pitch. Rob's specific enemy needs naming. The Staff Engineer background should be used, not listed.

### Vector 2: Anti-Template Homepage Copy
**Question:** What structural and tonal choices do websites praised for breaking convention make?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-2-anti-template-homepage-copy.md](iteration-1/vector-2-anti-template-homepage-copy.md)

- Analyzed 37signals, Stripe, SparkToro, Gumroad, Linear, Doist, Superhuman, MailChimp
- Core insight: they lead with a point of view, not a value proposition
- Stats sections, customer logos, "trusted by" banners are absent from every example. Stats-as-authority is the defining feature of *generic* consulting copy.
- Four alternative page flows identified: manifesto, narrative, product-as-proof, calm authority
- First person ("I") is standard for solo/small firms. "We" from one person is a lie the visitor can feel.

**Implications:** The v1 stats section is the exact anti-pattern. Page structure should follow voice, not convention. Opinions ARE the copy.

### Vector 3: How Expertise Communicates Without Credentials
**Question:** How do practitioners communicate competence through experience rather than claims?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-3-expertise-without-credentials.md](iteration-1/vector-3-expertise-without-credentials.md)

- Will Gadd (mountain guide), Ervin Somogyi (luthier), Yvon Chouinard (Patagonia), Gary Klein (decision research)
- 7 language patterns identified: mechanism over rule, the specific wrong, self-correction narratives, casual precision, named trade-offs, inclusive we, the scar not the wound
- Key finding: experts describe *what they noticed*, not *what methodology they applied*. "The snow had a hollow sound" not "we applied the extended column test."
- Foundation Stone already demonstrates these patterns ("on an ordinary Tuesday," "Speed is cheap")

**Implications:** The gap is between "warm competence" and "I've been here before." Current copy stays abstract where it should be specific. The Foundation Stone has the voice — the website lost it.

### Vector 4: Voice Discovery Methods
**Question:** How do brand strategists extract authentic voice from a founder?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-4-voice-discovery-methods.md](iteration-1/vector-4-voice-discovery-methods.md)

- Copyhackers, Brand Ventriloquism, GitPrime case study, POV Workshop methodology
- Gold standard: GitPrime co-founder said "Engineers love building cool shit" in a deep interview — that single phrase became ALL positioning
- Key method: layered questioning ("take me back..."), probing 3-4 levels deep until genuine conviction surfaces
- 5 specific exercises identified for the voice discovery session
- Critical pitfall: starting with adjectives instead of stories. Collect sentences first, derive guidelines second.

**Implications:** Session 1 should be structured interview, not brainstorm. Foundation Stone contains voice artifacts to mine. The "Say It Your Way" rewrite exercise is ideal for finding the gap.

## Synthesis

The four vectors converge on a single insight: **the voice already exists in the Foundation Stone, but the v1 website lost it by applying messaging guidelines mechanically instead of amplifying the founder's actual perspective.**

Three cross-cutting patterns:

**1. Specificity is the entire game.** Every vector independently arrived at: generic claims erode trust, specific experience builds it. Mountain guides describe what the snow sounds like, not the avalanche statistics. Successful founder sites tell stories about specific engagements, not capability summaries. Voice discovery methods work by mining for the one phrase no competitor would say. The v1 copy fails because it's specific about the *framework's features* but generic about the *founder's perspective*.

**2. The stats section is a red flag, not a trust signal.** V1's "Here's what we're seeing: 91% / 95% / 9%" section performs expertise through borrowed authority (citing others' research). Every anti-template site omits this pattern. Every practitioner-voice example replaces it with experience-based specificity. A guide doesn't quote the avalanche statistics — they tell you the snow sounded hollow this morning.

**3. Voice is found in artifacts, not guidelines.** The voice-and-tone doc (calm, knowledgeable, present, honest) correctly identifies the tonal zone. But ten different writers in that zone produce ten different voices. The Foundation Stone lines — "Speed is cheap," "on an ordinary Tuesday," "not as prophecy, but as pattern recognition" — are the actual voice artifacts. Session 1 should mine for more of these, not create new guidelines.

**The action is clear:** Run the voice discovery session as a structured interview (Exercise 2 from Vector 4) to surface Rob's specific convictions, frustrations, and experience stories. Use the Foundation Stone as the voice baseline. Test every sentence against the competitor-swap test. The trails concept is the strongest distinctive content available — lead with terrain, not with credentials.
