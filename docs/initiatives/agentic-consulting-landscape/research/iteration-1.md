### Vector 2: Business Models & Economics
**Question:** Which business models have viable unit economics? What's failed?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-2-business-models-and-economics.md](iteration-1/vector-2-business-models-and-economics.md)

**Key discoveries:**
- Outcome-based pricing is the dominant trend; 73% of clients prefer it over hourly. Intercom's Fin AI: $0.99/resolution, $1M→$100M+ ARR
- Solo+agents unit economics are exceptional: $100-500/mo tools, 70-85% margins, 5-8 clients manageable per solo operator
- AI literacy training: $895-$75,000 per engagement, 85-95% margins
- Developer rates dropped 36% in 3 years; pricing pressure is real but "speed premium" and quality perception offset it
- 95% of enterprise AI pilots fail; the "broken agency" model (one-off builds for <$2K clients) doesn't work
- Compound failure at scale: 95% per-step reliability × 20 steps = 36% success

**Implications:**
- Price for outcomes and speed, never hours
- Three-tier productized menu converts 35% better than custom pricing
- Human curation is both moat and cost center (budget 20-30% for review)
- YouTube pipeline economics are clear: $100-200/mo tools, 50% time savings

### Vector 3: Quality, Trust & Client Acquisition
**Question:** What's the real quality of agent output? How do operators build trust and find clients?
**Full report:** [iteration-1/vector-3-quality-trust-and-acquisition.md](iteration-1/vector-3-quality-trust-and-acquisition.md)

**Key discoveries:**
- AI code has 1.7x more defects than human code (470 PR study). Security issues 2.74x higher
- Deloitte's $290K Australian report: fabricated citations, non-existent sources. $1.6M Canadian report: same. Even Big Four fail at QA
- Overnight workforce is real but constrained: "It builds fast. It can't tell if what it built is good enough. I'm the filter." One runaway agent cost $47K over 11 days
- The Transparency Paradox (Schilke & Reimann 2025): disclosure reduces trust, but being caught hiding AI usage is worse. Proactive framing wins
- Twitter/X build-in-public: proven solo acquisition channel ($100K case study, zero cold outreach)
- 91% of C-suite executives admit faking AI knowledge — massive demand for honest consulting
- Insurers adding absolute AI exclusions to E&O policies effective Jan 2026. 73 new state AI laws in 2025

**Implications:**
- Position as the human quality filter, not the AI operator
- Disclose proactively with quality guarantees: "AI tools + human expert review on every deliverable"
- Avoid regulated industries until insurance and legal landscape stabilizes
- Content marketing with original insights (not AI-repackaged) is the acquisition flywheel

## Synthesis

Three cross-cutting patterns emerged that no single vector produced alone:

**1. The Governance Gap Is Real and Unoccupied.** Every successful solo operator independently arrived at the same primitive architecture: markdown instruction files → agent dispatch → human review. But nobody has structured governance — no lifecycle management, no behavioral constraints, no quality gates, no observable pipelines. They're all building the same house with different hammers, and none of them have blueprints. Sherpa's seven pillars (behavioral agents, governance engine, execution pipeline) directly address this gap. The consulting practice and the framework product reinforce each other: running agentic consulting validates the framework, and the framework makes the consulting more credible and more repeatable.

**2. The Quality-Trust-Pricing Triangle.** These three vectors are deeply entangled. Agent output quality (1.7x more code defects, Deloitte hallucinations) creates the trust problem. The trust problem creates the pricing pressure (36% developer rate decline, "paying for machine work" objection). The pricing pressure is solved by outcome-based billing (73% client preference), but outcome-based billing requires reliable quality to be sustainable. The operator who solves quality (through rigorous human review + governance) can charge outcome-based premiums. The operator who doesn't solve quality gets caught in the Deloitte trap — scaling output without scaling review.

**3. The Content-Consulting Flywheel Has a Shelf Life.** "Build in public" on Twitter/X is the proven solo acquisition channel today ($100K case study). But 85% of marketers now use AI for content, and AI search systems are learning to ignore repackaged content. The flywheel that works in 2026 is: original insights and frameworks → content distribution → client acquisition. This means Sherpa's research artifacts (like this initiative) aren't just internal — they're the raw material for the content engine that drives client acquisition. The framework's governance artifacts become the thought leadership.

**Single most important insight:** The market is in a unique window where (a) the unit economics of solo+agents are proven (70-85% margins), (b) no governance tooling exists, and (c) demand for honest AI consulting is massive (91% C-suite faking). A solo operator who ships both the consulting practice AND the governance framework captures both sides of the market simultaneously.

## Open Questions for Next Iteration

1. **Review overhead ratio** — What percentage of time savings from overnight agents gets consumed by morning review? No systematic measurement exists. This determines whether overnight dispatch is net-positive or break-even. Directly impacts `overnight-dispatch` initiative.

2. **Insurance and liability playbook** — How should a solo AI consultant structure their business entity, insurance, and client contracts to manage AI liability? Insurers are excluding AI; 73 new state laws are creating exposure. Need concrete legal/business structure recommendations.

3. **Content engine design** — What specific content formats, posting cadences, and platform strategies work for AI consulting content marketing in 2026? The generic "build in public" advice needs operationalization. Ties directly to `blog-content-engine` and `headless-youtube-pipeline` initiatives.

4. **Practitioner depth interviews** — The named operators (Bilsborough, Setas, Saraev, Tomar) have public content but we haven't traced their full workflows. What does a week look like? What's the actual client delivery flow? What tools do they reach for at each step?

5. **Sherpa-as-product positioning** — Is the framework better positioned as consulting infrastructure (used internally, never mentioned to clients) or as a visible differentiator ("our governance framework ensures quality")? The transparency paradox research suggests the answer may vary by client segment.
