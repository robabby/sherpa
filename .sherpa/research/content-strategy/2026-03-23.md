## Platform Analysis

### LinkedIn 2026: The Algorithm Grew Up

The most important finding tonight. LinkedIn's algorithm has undergone a genuine shift — not minor tuning, but a philosophical reorientation.

**What changed:**
- Organic reach has dropped ~50% YoY as AI-generated noise saturates the feed
- The algorithm now penalizes "engagement bait" (polls, tag-fests, provocative one-liners) and shallow anecdotes
- The new signals: **Dwell Time** (how long someone stops scrolling to read) and **Conversation Depth** (quality of comments, not quantity)
- A post with 50 "Great post!" comments is worth *less* than a post with 10 paragraph-long debates
- "Bro-poetry" (single-sentence paragraphs, fake suspense, melodramatic personal stories) is actively flagged as low-quality

**What wins:**
- Deep expert narratives — detailed, opinionated, practitioner-backed
- Stories that force the reader to slow down and read to find the resolution
- Posts that prompt people to share *their own experiences* in the comments
- "Knowledge exchange" framing: posts teach something concrete, not just signal identity

**Personal profiles vs. company pages:**
- Employee/personal content reaches **561% further** than company page posts (Clearly Linked data)
- People trust people — a human practitioner story is infinitely more compelling than a product announcement

**Why this is good for Rob:**
Rob's war stories — 472 PRs, 6 Swift apps, real failures and how he debugged them — are exactly what dwell time rewards. Nobody skims through "we tried agentic engineering and here's what we actually hit." They slow down, they read, they comment with their own experiences. That's the algorithm signal LinkedIn is now prioritizing.

The engineers churning out generic "AI will replace jobs" hot takes are being penalized. Rob has specifics. Specifics win in this environment.

**Key formatting recommendations for LinkedIn (2026):**
- Lead with tension/problem, not a question (questions feel like engagement bait)
- Use "line breaks that create momentum, not artificial suspense"
- Target 150-300 words for text posts; carousels can go longer with visual pacing
- Include one specific number or data point in the first three lines
- End with an open question that invites professional debate (not "agree/disagree?")

### HackerNews: What Actually Gets to the Front Page

Synthesized from a Writer's Guide to HN and analysis of 2025's top-performing posts.

**Topic categories that consistently land:**
- **Tech history** — "How [well-known thing] actually came to be" — obscure origin stories, the stranger the better
- **Hot takes with substance** — slightly contrarian, but backed by lived experience not just opinion
- **Data-driven posts** — research with actual numbers most people don't know; the finding needs to be genuinely surprising
- **Honest war stories about building things** — bugs, constraints, unexpected failures, and how they were resolved
- **Projects that scratch a geeky itch** — even impractical things get love if they're clever and technically interesting
- **"Get off my lawn" tech industry takes** — RSS should have survived, apps should be more repairable, etc.

**What doesn't work on HN:**
- Product launches / promotional content (~4% front page rate for corporate blog posts)
- Blog posts about ideas without data or technical depth
- Anything that reads like it was optimized for SEO first

**Title mechanics (this is real):**
- The title IS the thumbnail — it has to survive a scan in 0.2 seconds
- Best-performing titles are either very specific ("I went from 0 to 10K upvotes by changing one thing in my post") or genuinely intriguing/curious ("Why syncing is harder than it looks")
- Trust your own "Curiosity Barometer" — if it surprised you, it will surprise others
- Avoid clickbait: HN readers will downvote deliberately mysterious titles if the article doesn't deliver

**The 1-in-10 rule:**
Don't expect every post to land on HN front page. A realistic success rate is 1 in 10. The strategy is publishing consistently, not engineering virality. Simon Willison's dominance comes from volume (1,000+ posts in 2025) + quality on the big pieces.

**Show HN for Rob:**
The "Show HN" format could be excellent for Sherpa Studio if it's genuinely runnable/tryable. "Show HN: I built a git-native context system for agentic development workflows" — but only if it's interactive, not just a read. HN guideline: Show HN is for things people can run or hold, not blog posts.

### The Willison Model: Link Blog + Deep Dives

Simon Willison was #1 most popular HN blogger in 2025 — for the third straight year. The analysis of why is directly applicable to Rob:

**What Willison does differently:**
1. **Not selling anything** — writes as a power user, not a vendor. No allegiance to tools = authority
2. **Volume + depth combination** — 1,000+ posts in 2025, but with full-length articles mixed in (118 substantial pieces)
3. **Brings walled-garden content to open web** — "I'm worried they put co-pilot in Excel" was just a TikTok quote + commentary. Low effort, high HN value because HN *can't link to TikTok*
4. **Link blog as a practice** — Short quotes + a paragraph of honest commentary. "Low effort, high value way to contribute to internet life at large"

**The Jeff Geerling corollary:**
Geerling was #2 on HN in 2025. His pattern: YouTube first, then companion blog post with more detail. YouTube videos rarely succeed on HN directly, but the blog post version does. He said it himself: "I still try to post a companion blog post (usually with more detail, and updated over time) for any video I publish."

**The combined model Rob should consider:**
- Blog/robabby.com: home base, where the deep stuff lives
- LinkedIn: practitioner stories in native post format (dwell time content)
- HN: submitted as blog posts, optimized title, genuine data/insight
- YouTube/video: companion pieces for the blog, not standalone content
- Link blog angle: even quick commentary on industry news can drive consistent HN presence

## Content That's Working

### The "Structured Story Arc" Format on LinkedIn

The 2026 algorithm rewards a specific narrative structure. Breaking down what works:

```
Opening: A specific moment of tension (not a question)
"11 weeks ago I pushed my 472nd PR with an AI coding agent.
What I found in that commit surprised me."

Middle: The earned insight — what you discovered, what went wrong, what changed
[200-300 words with real specifics, numbered list of learnings]

Close: An open question that invites professional experience-sharing
"If you've worked with agentic workflows at scale, what constraint
hit you first?"
```

The key is "earned insight" — the reader needs to feel like the lesson came from real experience, not a LinkedIn ghost-writer. Rob has genuine war stories. He should write them in his own voice, not cleaned up.

### The "Specific Number + Unexpected Angle" Title Formula

Works across HN, LinkedIn, and blog posts:

- "472 PRs, 6 Swift apps, 11 weeks — and the moment I almost burned it all down" *(personal arc)*
- "Why agentic engineering broke my code review process (and what I built instead)" *(problem/solution)*
- "The constraint nobody talks about in AI-assisted development" *(mystery + insider knowledge)*
- "What 11 weeks of 40+ commits/day taught me about human-AI collaboration" *(number + unexpected scale)*

The number is doing two jobs: it signals *this is real* and it creates instant scale context. Readers think "40 commits a day?!" before they've even decided to read.

## People Doing It Well

### Simon Willison (simonwillison.net)
*Previously mentioned — going deeper tonight*

**The link blog mechanic Rob should steal:** Willison publishes short "TIL" (Today I Learned) posts constantly. Each one is a paragraph or two. They're indexed, searchable, build his topic density on HN, and occasionally go viral on their own. He treats blogging as a *practice*, not an event.

**Rob's TIL equivalent:** "TIL that Claude Code handles ambiguous function signatures differently than I expected when there's no type annotation" — two paragraphs, posted to robabby.com, submitted to HN as a link. Low effort. Builds volume. Creates a trail that signals expertise over time.

Willison recently (Feb 23, 2026) published "Writing about Agentic Engineering Patterns" — Rob's content area, validated by the most-followed HN blogger. This is confirmation that the topic has HN legs right now.

### Jeff Geerling (jeffgeerling.com)
**The companion post strategy:** Geerling writes YouTube video scripts and then translates them into blog posts with more detail, updated over time. The blog post gets HN traction; the YouTube video gets long-term search. He treats the two as *different products* from the same research, not the same content in different containers.

**For Rob:** A 10-minute "Agentic Engineering Workflow" YouTube video generates one YouTube artifact and one blog post artifact. Both have independent distribution. Neither cannibalizes the other.

### Daniel Wirtz (danielwirtz.com)
*Reverse-engineered his #1 HN post (100K readers in a week)*

His principle: **Trust your own Curiosity Barometer.** If something genuinely surprised you, assume it will surprise similar readers. Don't optimize for what you think people want — write from genuine curiosity.

His viral post was about a "spot the difference" optical trick — not technically impressive, but *genuinely surprising* to him. The authenticity of the curiosity translated to 100K readers.

**Applied to Rob:** The specific moment when something in the agentic workflow surprised Rob — that's the post. Not "here are 7 lessons from 472 PRs." Instead: "Something broke in my agentic workflow that I didn't expect, and understanding why changed how I think about AI coding agents."

### New: LinkedIn Post Sequence (Content Calendar Prototype)

Based on the 2026 algorithm requirements, here's a proposed 2-week posting sequence that mixes formats:

**Week 1:**
- **Monday** (text post, dwell-time story): "The moment my agentic workflow broke in a way I didn't expect" — hook on problem, earned insight, open question
- **Wednesday** (carousel document): "5 constraints nobody talks about in AI-assisted development" — each slide = one constraint, visual pacing keeps dwell time high
- **Friday** (short text, personal): Behind the scenes of what it's actually like to push 40+ commits in a day with an AI collaborator — humanizing, conversational

**Week 2:**
- **Tuesday** (carousel): "Before/After: How my code review process changed after 472 PRs with Claude" — before/after slides perform well
- **Thursday** (text post, data-driven): One surprising metric or finding from the 11 weeks — single specific number + what it means
- **Saturday optional** (link post): Share a relevant article with your own 3-sentence take — Willison's link blog model, low effort

**The principle:** Never post the same format twice in a row. Mix text/carousel/video so the algorithm can test what your audience engages with.

### New: Submit to HN After Publishing

After each substantive blog post:
1. Submit the exact blog post URL to news.ycombinator.com
2. Title = exact blog post title (do not change it)
3. Don't DM/email people asking for upvotes — that gets flagged
4. Engage with every comment for the first 2-3 hours (algorithm rewards dwell time + commenter response cycles)
5. Target: 1 in 10 submissions landing on front page is a success rate
