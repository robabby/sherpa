There's a problem that every team building with AI runs into, usually around week two: the markdown piles up. Conventions, agent definitions, research docs, task specs, review gates — each one reasonable in isolation, collectively unmanageable. No structure. No lifecycle. No way to know which context is stale. Most teams just let it accumulate and wonder why their agents keep going sideways.

That's the problem I set out to solve with Sherpa Studio. What started as a personal framework for organizing AI development artifacts grew into a full agentic engineering platform: multi-agent dispatch across nine backends, behavioral agent definitions, an MCP server for context injection, an initiative lifecycle engine, and a knowledge management layer that keeps everything coherent as projects evolve. I didn't prototype it — I used it to build production products.

One of those products is WavePoint, an astrology platform I built solo: a Next.js web app, six native Apple apps, and an MCP server, all shipped in eleven weeks across 472+ pull requests. The pace was only possible because the scaffolding held.

Before these projects, I spent several years as a Staff Frontend Engineer at PartySlate, joining when the team was twelve people and leaving after we'd grown to fifty-plus, closed a Series B, and posted 258% year-over-year revenue growth. That experience gave me a clear picture of what it means to build infrastructure that scales with a company, not just with the codebase.

I've been watching Linear's AI direction closely. The framing on linear.app/now — that real progress comes when AI stops requiring constant input and proactively moves work forward — is exactly the thesis I've been building toward. I noticed you recently expanded the MCP server to support initiatives, project milestones, and updates from tools like Cursor and Claude. That's the same surface area I've been working in: making structured context available to agents so they can operate with confidence instead of guessing. The initiative lifecycle engine in Sherpa Studio and Linear's initiatives feature are solving adjacent versions of the same coordination problem.

Karri's point about the "move fast and break things" playbook being played out resonates with me. I care about craft in the same way — not as a slower path, but as the only path that actually compounds. Sherpa's foundation stone values are honesty, craftsmanship, empowerment, integrity, and community. They weren't marketing copy. They shaped every technical decision.

I'm a strong match for the Senior/Staff Product Engineer role. I've been writing React and TypeScript for years, I'm comfortable owning product surface end-to-end, and I'm the kind of engineer who reads changelog posts on a Tuesday night because I actually care how the product works.

I'd welcome the paid work trial format. I think it's the right way to evaluate this kind of fit, and I'm confident in what it would show.

— Rob Abby
