## Version 1: Formal Application

Rob Abby · robabby23@gmail.com · Bellingham, WA (Remote)

Vercel's blog post on Design Engineering gets something exactly right: the role is about "shifting from owning a specific part of the process to owning results." That framing describes the last 15 years of my career better than any job title I've held.

I'm applying for the Design Engineer, Product role.

My through-line is design systems — not as a deliverable, but as infrastructure. At SAVO, I stood up the company's first design system from scratch, running 50+ user research sessions to ground it in actual behavior before writing a line of component code. At PartySlate, I built a 25+ component library with full Storybook integration and drove Lighthouse accessibility scores from 78 to 95. Most recently, I built WavePoint — a Next.js platform backed by six native Apple apps — around a cross-platform design system I called "Modern Mystic": Radix UI and Tailwind on the web, SwiftUI primitives on native, one coherent visual language across seven surfaces. 472 PRs in 11 weeks, solo.

The AI dimension entered when I built Sherpa Studio. It started from a specific friction: AI-assisted development generates enormous amounts of structured markdown, and no tool was built to handle it at scale. I built the scaffolding — a collaboration framework that organizes, surfaces, and routes that output into usable structure. That project pushed me from design-systems thinking into infrastructure thinking, and the two are harder to separate than I expected.

What draws me to Vercel specifically is the Geist system. It's one of the cleanest examples of a design system that doesn't just document components — it *is* the product experience. The interactive docs playground, the font specimen page, the design team site as a proving ground for new web techniques — these are exactly the kinds of projects I want to be building. Not as polished artifacts handed off to engineers, but as shipped product.

I work well embedded in product teams, and I work well with enough autonomy to define the problem. The Design Engineering model at Vercel — skip the handoff, iterate in code, own the outcome — is how I've always preferred to work. I just haven't always had the title that matched it.

I'd love to talk.

— Rob Abby
