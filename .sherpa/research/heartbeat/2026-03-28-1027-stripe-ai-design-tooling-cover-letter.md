## Cover Letter

There's a specific problem I've been thinking about for the past year: how do you build tooling that makes AI a genuine collaborator in the design-to-code workflow — not a shortcut that trades quality for speed, but infrastructure that actually raises the bar?

That's what led me to build Sherpa Studio, an agentic engineering workflow suite. It's not a wrapper around an LLM. It's a system designed around how engineers and AI should work together — with structured workflows, quality gates, and the kind of opinionated architecture that makes the output consistently good rather than occasionally impressive. I built it solo, alongside WavePoint (a Next.js platform with six native Apple apps), shipping 472 pull requests in 11 weeks. The velocity came from the tooling, not despite it.

What makes this role compelling is that Stripe is solving a version of this problem at a scale I haven't seen elsewhere. The AI-powered design system migration — moving 100% of the Dashboard codebase to the latest design system version — is exactly the kind of high-leverage, infrastructure-level AI application that I believe matters most. Not AI generating UI from scratch, but AI helping maintain consistency, accelerate migrations, and reduce the friction between design intent and production code.

My background: 13+ years as a frontend engineer, most recently Staff level. I've built and maintained component systems across multiple product surfaces, designed monorepo architectures that support rapid iteration, and spent the last year living at the intersection of AI tooling and frontend craft. With Sherpa, I wasn't just using AI to code faster — I was designing the workflows, constraints, and feedback loops that make AI-assisted engineering reliable.

The gap between "AI can generate a component" and "AI can help maintain a design system at Stripe's scale" is enormous. I've been working in that gap. I'd welcome the chance to talk about what I've learned.
