# Resume Project Framing for Non-Founders

*Research: 2026-03-24 10:00 PM PT — Luna heartbeat research*

## The Question

How do experienced engineers (Staff/Senior, 10+ years) present independent projects on resumes and LinkedIn when those projects are NOT startups — they're demonstrations of skill?

---

## 1. Title Options (Not "Founder")

Strongest options for solo technical projects at senior level:

- **"Lead Engineer"** — implies technical ownership and architecture decisions without implying a company
- **"Sole Engineer"** — clean, direct, signals full-stack depth and breadth of ownership
- **"Independent Engineer"** — bridges project + consulting positioning
- **LinkedIn-only:** "Builder" or "Creator" work in headlines as identity signals, but too vague for resume bullets

**Cleanest move:** Treat each project like a job entry. Project name as the "employer." Role = actual technical function.

---

## 2. Where Projects Go on the Resume

Three options, ranked by strength for Rob's situation:

1. **In the Experience section (recommended)** — Format exactly like a job. Project name = company. Strongest when the projects ARE the recent work and there's a gap to address. Prevents them from reading as an afterthought.
2. **Separate "Independent Projects" section** — Good when employment history is intact and projects are additive. Labeled: "Independent Projects," "Selected Builds," "Technical Portfolio."
3. **Narrative summary only** — Useful for cover letters and LinkedIn About, not a substitute for bullet points.

**For Rob: Option 1, without hesitation.** The projects are the most recent and most relevant work.

---

## 3. Real Examples From the Community

- **r/cscareerquestions (hiring manager quote):** *"The hiring manager explicitly said that the only reason I was considered is a personal project of mine, and it was brought up in other interviews as well."*
- **Software Engineering StackExchange:** *"I would record the year under a separate section titled 'Sabbatical', with each interesting project listed. If you're good at what you do, a year off won't hurt."*
- **Resume advice consensus:** "Independent Projects" is the standard section label; format identically to work experience with dates, role, and achievement bullets.

---

## 4. Framing Language That Works

**Words to avoid:** "side project," "passion project," "personal project," "hobby," "experimenting with"

**What works instead:**

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Personal project: built web app" | "Architected and shipped [Name] — production-grade platform..." |
| "Side project using Next.js" | "Solo-engineered full-stack platform: 472+ PRs in 11 weeks..." |
| "Experimenting with AI" | "Designed AI-native tooling from greenfield to production..." |

The "solo" angle is actually a **multiplier** at staff level — doing alone what normally requires a team is a *signal*, not a stigma. Lead into it: *"As the sole engineer, owned all layers: architecture, implementation, deployment, and iteration."*

Metrics are critical for credibility: **472 PRs in 11 weeks**, **6 native Swift apps**, **MCP server**, **deployed + accessible** — these lift the work from "hobby" to "production."

---

## 5. How Target Companies Actually Evaluate This

**Linear:** Uses paid work trials. Explicitly values self-direction, craft, and shipping quality work. A solo project with 472 PRs is arguably *better signal* than a year at a mediocre large company. They care about what you made, not who you worked for.

**Vercel:** Recruits "Design Engineers" — people who cross domains. They value cross-domain technical fluency and "iterate to greatness" shipping velocity. Someone who built a Next.js platform solo *then* shipped 6 native Swift apps demonstrates exactly the range they want.

**Stripe:** More rigorous — cares about technical depth and systems thinking. Lean into architecture decisions, not just output velocity.

**General finding:** For craft-first companies (Linear, Vercel, Ashby, Retool), solo project work is **legitimate employment history if presented with conviction and evidence.** They're not asking "why weren't you at a real company?" — they're asking "can you build something excellent?"

---

## Summary Recommendations

- **Resume:** Put Sherpa Studio and WavePoint directly in the Experience section as job entries. Use "Lead Engineer" or "Sole Engineer" as the title. Lead bullets with architecture, velocity, and outcomes — not technology lists.
- **LinkedIn headline options:** *"Staff Engineer · Built Sherpa Studio (AI dev tooling) + WavePoint (6-app Apple platform) solo"*
- **Cover letter:** The "I built this to prove I could" framing reads as self-aware and confident. On the resume, let the evidence speak.
- **Biggest risk:** Underselling. These projects demonstrate exactly the skills that Linear, Vercel, and Ashby want. Don't bury them.
